Introduction

Considering everything that has occurred in the most recent years, it might seem like a somewhat grim and unforgiving time in the world. With a worldwide pandemic going on for already almost 3 years, and more recent developments in Ukraine, many would think that the world has seen better days. However, is it really true that the world is getting darker and gloomier with each passing year? Every year, the United Nations Sustainable Development Solutions Network delivers the World Happiness Report, a collection of articles and rankings of national happiness as a whole based on responses from polling. It uses a variety of quality of life factors, in addition to polled responses, to quantify happiness around the world. While it could be argued that happiness is difficult to quantify, if we want to know if the world truly is more or less happy than it was some years ago, then it is necessary to do so.

The datasets were obtained from Kaggle.com(link: https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/mathurinache/world-happiness-report). Its columns include country, happiness ranking, a happiness score from 1 to 10, GDP per capita, average family size, and standardized measures of freedom to make life choices, government trust by perceptions of corruption, generosity, and dystopia residual. In this tutorial, I will primarily be focusing on the relationship between happiness score and year, as the main focus is to see if happiness has increased over time or not, and testing it against my own hypothesis, which, as an optimist, is that the world has gotten happier in general over time. To do this, I will use Python 3 and its libraries pandas, numpy, scikit-learn, and matplotlib to manage the data and calculate the necessary statistics to find the trend of happiness over time.

Pandas: https://pandas.pydata.org/ Numpy: https://numpy.org/doc/stable/reference/index.html#reference Scikit-Learn: https://scikit-learn.org/stable/ Matplotlib: https://matplotlib.org/

Part 4: Hypothesis Testing

The main hypothesis that I want to test is that happiness has increased over time, on average. This can easily be tested via fitting a linear regression model for happiness and year, with methods provided by the Scikit-Learn library.

In [25]:
years2 = data.year.array
years3 = np.reshape(years2, (-1, 1))
scores = data['score']
reg = LinearRegression().fit(years3, scores)
prediction = reg.predict(years3)
plt.plot(years2, prediction, color = "blue")
print(reg.coef_)

[0.02494312]

While the slope coefficient of the model seems to show a positive number, a proper hypothesis test is in order to determine if it really is a significant positive linear relationship. Due to how small the slope coefficient is, I can use a null hypothesis of there not being a significant linear relationship(that is, difference between happiness score in 2015 vs. 2021 should be 0). Then, using Scipy(link: https://docs.scipy.org/doc/scipy/index.html) and its ttest_ind method, I perform a two sample t-test, comparing the difference between highest and lowest values on the regression line versus the null difference of 0, with an alpha value of 0.05, meaning that the null hypothesis is rejected with a p value less than 0.05.

In [26]:
from scipy import stats
samp1 = data.loc[data['year'] == 2015, ['score']]
samp2 = data.loc[data['year'] == 2021, ['score']]
t = stats.ttest_ind(samp1, samp2)
print(t.statistic)
print(t.pvalue)

[-1.23821191]
[0.21658971]

From the p value, it is clear that we fail to reject the null hypothesis, meaning that there is actually no significant linear relationship between time and happiness.

Part 5: Insights gained from performing analysis

There are obviously many different factors that influence world happiness beyond the two that I was able to cover. Different nations and regions very likely are affected differently by different factors that could affect happiness, and not every one will even follow the same trends of happiness relative to other parameters. For example, there could be a country ruled by a totalitarian dictatorship with high happiness overall despite having no freedom of life choices. However, overall, happiness does appear to be slowly increasing, or at least remaining stable.

When looking at freedom of life choices and perceptions of corruption overall, the fact that perceptions of corruption appear to be increasing faster than freedom may explain why the gain in happiness score per year is lower. Since people believe that the government is getting more corrupt than they are becoming more free to live their lives, happiness may increase more slowly than if they felt more free every year. However, this may not be accurate, as it could just be coincidental. Further research on the topic would certainly be on the other factors listed on the datasets relating to the happiness score, like GDP per capita and social support.
